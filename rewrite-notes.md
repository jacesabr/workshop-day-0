## A. Principles — how the whole deck should feel

1. **Non-boring conveyance of information.** Information arrives through a hook, not a bullet list.
2. **Engage via questions.** Questions are the primary engine of the deck.
3. Two kinds of question, used deliberately:
   - **Informational** — the audience learns the point *from the answer* ("What can AI actually do now?").
   - **Transitive** — the question's only job is to pull you into the next slide ("So what would you do with five free minutes?").
4. **Slides are speaker cues, not a script.** The deck is talking points for *me* to engage the room and tangent off — short prompts, minimal on-screen text. Never paragraphs. Each slide = a headline + 1–3 cue lines + a question I can open up.

## B. The opening — outcomes first

> Note: *"start with the outcomes, who we want them to be, and who we want them to not be."*

- Open on the **two outcomes**, as a fork the audience has to pick between:
  - **Who we want you to become** — understands the tech around you and bends it to make your life/work better (the "with AI" person; current deck calls this main-character era).
  - **Who we want you to NOT be** — still operating with a pre-AI understanding of the world; left behind in ~5 years.
- This replaces / precedes the current cold open ("Hello, World… you're cooked").

> Note: *"make the case: singular use case → expectations for future workers."*

- The throughline from open to close: start from **one concrete thing one person did**, then widen to **"this is becoming the baseline expectation for anyone who works."** The examples are the rungs; the expectation is the top of the ladder.

## C. Engagement devices (reusable mechanics to thread through slides)

1. **"What do you understand from this picture?"** — Socratic image-read. Show an image/illustration, ask the room what they read from it, *then* reveal the point. Good fit for the existing SVG illustrations.
2. **Engage in the problem statement.** Don't state the problem — put them inside it. Example given: *listening to music, bored, skipping songs, nothing hits.* Each "before" scenario should feel like this.
3. **Tool reframe.** Every tool is introduced as: *"use this to present or explain something that you'd otherwise have to do yourself."* The tool does the work you can't / won't do manually.

### F. The barrier-is-gone beat (KEPT — slides 08 & 09)

Both old slides survive, reframed as **audience-guess questions** (informational: they learn from the answer).

**Slide 08 — You don't need to know the tech.**
- Ask: **"How much of this do you think you actually need to understand to build something real?"** (let them guess)
- Reveal: **none of the names.** Postgres vs MySQL, database tables, auth, storage/regions, "serverless", a CS degree — describe what you want, AI picks fine (for yourself, friends, up to ~1,000 users).

**Slide 09 — Free to build.**
- Ask: **"What do you think it costs to build and run a real app?"** (let them throw out numbers)
- Reveal: **$0.** Every layer — AI, coding, hosting, database — has a free tier in 2026. Idea → live product used by ~1,000 people, zero dollars. The bar to start is gone; most people just don't know it yet.
